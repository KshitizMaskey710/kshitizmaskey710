## The leadership gap

A prospective lead was introduced to a development team with the expectation that he would guide its work. His background was in accounts and business operations, not software delivery, and he had no prior working knowledge of Agile, Scrum, SDLC, Product Ownership, or the rhythm of managing a development team.

The challenge was not commitment or capability. It was the gap between useful existing business experience and a new software leadership responsibility.

## Why Product Owner awareness mattered

His accounts background already offered a foundation for thinking about value, priorities, stakeholders, and tradeoffs. Product Owner concepts helped connect that experience to outcome definition, backlog ordering, acceptance, and communication with a development team.

## A practical learning path

The training began with the software development lifecycle, then introduced Agile principles and the Scrum framework. Accountabilities, events, artifacts, and the relationship between product decisions and team execution were explained using scenarios rather than abstract definitions.

Microsoft 365 tools were included as a coordination layer: where decisions could be recorded, work made visible, documents managed, and communication organized. Tools were positioned as support for an operating rhythm, not a substitute for one.

## Outcome

The participant developed a clearer mental model of how software work moves from need to delivery, how a team collaborates, and where a business-facing lead can contribute without pretending to be the technical authority.

> A non-technical leader does not need to become an engineer. They need enough delivery understanding to ask useful questions, support decisions, and create clarity around value.

## Key lesson

Training should start from the learner’s existing strengths. Translating familiar business concepts into delivery concepts creates confidence faster than beginning with framework vocabulary alone.
